Understanding Lucky Day Traps

What is a Lucky Day Trap?

A lucky day trap occurs when most of the total effect in an experiment comes from a single period (day, week, or month). This creates a misleading impression of the true treatment effect.

Why This Matters

Why Lucky Days Are Problematic:

  • • Misleading results - A single period can drive apparent significance
  • • Unreliable inference - Results may not generalize beyond that period
  • • External factors - The effect could be due to external events rather than the treatment
  • • False confidence - Decisions based on lucky days often fail in production
  • • Noise vs. signal - Random concentration of effect doesn't indicate true treatment impact

How We Detect Lucky Days

Detection Criteria:

A period is flagged as a lucky day when:

  • • For higher-is-better experiments: The variant's conversion rate is more than 2.5x the base's conversion rate on that period
  • • For lower-is-better experiments: The base's conversion rate is more than 2.5x the variant's conversion rate on that period

When a lucky day is detected, we calculate what the results would look like after excluding that period to understand the true underlying effect.

Common Causes

External Factors

  • • Seasonal events or holidays
  • • Marketing campaigns or promotions
  • • News events affecting traffic
  • • Technical issues or outages
  • • Competitor actions

Statistical Variation

  • • Random concentration of effect
  • • Small sample sizes in some periods
  • • Natural variance in user behavior
  • • Regression to the mean
  • • Sampling variability

What To Do

1. Exclude and Reanalyze

Remove the lucky day period and recalculate the experiment results. Check if the effect remains significant and substantial.

2. Investigate the Cause

Look for external factors that might explain the spike: promotions, events, technical issues, or other changes.

3. Validate the Results

If excluding the lucky day makes the result non-significant, treat the results as untrustworthy and rerun the experiment.

4. Proceed with Caution

If the result remains significant after excluding the lucky day, proceed but validate with a follow-up run to confirm the effect persists.

5. Monitor Closely

If you decide to ship based on results with a lucky day, monitor closely in production to ensure the effect holds.

Remember

Lucky days are a warning sign that results may not be trustworthy. Always validate results that depend heavily on a single period, especially when excluding that period changes the conclusion.
