Understanding Twyman's Law

What is Twyman's Law?

"Any figure that looks interesting or different is usually wrong." - Twyman's Law suggests that results that appear "too good to be true" often indicate underlying problems.

Why This Matters

Suspiciously Low P-values Indicate:

  • • Data quality issues - Problems with data collection or processing
  • • P-hacking - Cherry-picking results or multiple testing without correction
  • • Selection bias - Non-representative samples or biased data
  • • Technical errors - Bugs in statistical calculations or data handling
  • • Fabricated data - In extreme cases, data may be manipulated

What Makes a P-value Suspicious?

Warning signs of "too good to be true" results:

  • • P-values with 6+ zeros (e.g., p < 0.000001)
  • • Unrealistically large effect sizes - Effects that seem too dramatic
  • • Perfect statistical significance - Results that are "too clean"
  • • Inconsistent with prior knowledge - Results that contradict established patterns

Common Causes

Statistical Issues

  • • Multiple testing without correction
  • • Data dredging or fishing
  • • Incorrect statistical models
  • • Violation of test assumptions
  • • Overfitting to the data

Data Quality Issues

  • • Biased data collection
  • • Non-random sampling
  • • Data preprocessing errors
  • • Outlier manipulation
  • • Missing data handling issues

What To Do

1. Investigate Thoroughly

Examine your data collection process, statistical methods, and any recent changes that might explain the suspicious results.

2. Validate Your Data

Check for data quality issues, outliers, and ensure your statistical assumptions are met.

3. Replicate the Analysis

Re-run your analysis with fresh data or different time periods to see if the results hold.

4. Apply Multiple Testing Corrections

If you've run multiple tests, apply appropriate corrections (Bonferroni, FDR, etc.) to control for false positives.

5. Seek Independent Review

Have someone else review your methodology and results to identify potential issues you might have missed.

Remember

Twyman's Law is a valuable reminder that extraordinary results require extraordinary evidence. Always be skeptical of results that seem too good to be true.
